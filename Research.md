## Population genomic structure of sorghum landraces across landscape, environment and culture

Sorghum is a staple crop consumed by billions of people globally. Understanding the factors that influence dispersal patterns of staple crops may allow us to predict how barriers to gene flow, like climate, alter the frequency and distribution of genomic diversity across space, particularly under temporal, environmental and habitat variability.In this study we take an integrative approach in our assesement of sorghum population structure by modeling gene flow in the context of both geographic space and human movement (travel time). We also consider the degree to which climate, space and language explain genetic diversity and shape the pattern of geographic structuring. By utilizing identity by descent segments, we assess spatiotemporal differences in the degree of shared ancestry and pattern of genetic relatedness across space, which has not been widely implemented in crop landscape genetics. We also develop a novel implementation of pairwise haplotype sharing with redundancy analysis (RDA) to determine how the importance of different factors explaining geographic population structure has changed over time.

## Understanding the evolutionary processes underlying trichome dimorphism in Arabidopsis thaliana

Genetic variation in natural plant populations is essential for adaptation to variable environments through space and time. By understanding the evolutionary processes that maintain genetic variation, we can make predictions about how species respond to novel selective pressures. Many plant species exhibit striking genetic variation in the abundance of leaf hairs (trichomes) with many examples of presence/absence dimorphism. Here, we test the relationship between GL1 and trichome dimorphism and assess the evolutionary processes acting on the GL1 locus to maintain this ecologically important trait in natural Arabidopsis thaliana populations.
