## Population genomic structure of sorghum landraces across landscape, environment and space

Sorghum is a staple crop consumed by billions of people globally. Recent studies have investigated the patterns of early diffusion of sorghum across space as well as the genetic mechanisms driving adaptation to diverse environments. However, the processes driving differences in spatial structuring and the relative effect of influential factors like climate, geographic space, human movement and language across different spatial and temporal scales are not well understood in sorghum. 

Here we take an integrative approach in our assessment of spatial population genomic structure in globally distributed sorghum landraces by modeling gene flow in the context of both geographic space and human movement (travel time). In addition to climate and space, we also consider the degree to which language explains genetic diversity and shapes the pattern of geographic structuring. By utilizing identity by descent segments, we assess spatiotemporal differences in the degree of shared ancestry and pattern of genetic relatedness across space, which has not been widely implemented in crop landscape genetics. We develop a novel implementation of pairwise haplotype sharing with redundancy analysis (RDA) to determine how the importance of different factors explaining geographic population structure has changed over time.

We found strong evidence of isolation by distance in sorghum in both models representing geographic space and travel time. After geographic distance, language families explained the most SNP variation and genetic relatedness across time. In our assessment of geographic ancestry across different timescales, we observed regional and temporal differences in both the degree of shared ancestry and the rate of geographic decay over time.
